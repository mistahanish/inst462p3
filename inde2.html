Chapter 01 — Before the Disc

A world of vinyl and tape

Before the CD existed, the music industry was a battle of formats. Vinyl dominated the 1970s. 8-track cartridges surged, then died. By the early 1980s, the cassette and the Walkman had made portable music a reality for a generation.

Then in 1982, a silver disc changed everything.

Vinyl ~50%+ share through 1984 Cassette overtook Vinyl in 1984 8-Track dead by 1982

Chapter 02 — The Silver Invasion

The CD takes over

When Sony and Philips launched the compact disc in 1982, it was a curiosity. By 1987 it had overtaken vinyl. By 1991 it outsold cassettes. Labels re-sold entire back catalogs to fans who already owned the albums — on CD.

Chapter 03 — The Peak

2000: $13 billion on discs alone

By 2002 the CD had captured nearly all physical music revenue — the most dominant any single format has ever been in U.S. history. The industry was making obscene money. Then a college dropout named Shawn Fanning launched Napster.

CD peak: $13.2B (2000) 2002 — near-total dominance Napster launched: June 1999

Chapter 04 — Three Killers

What killed the disc

The CD's collapse wasn't one event — it was a cascade. Three technologies stripped away the economics that made CDs so profitable.

1999

Napster

Peer-to-peer sharing made music free. CD sales fell 20% in two years.

2003

iTunes Store

At $0.99 per song, not $15 per album. The album era ended.

2011

Spotify US

All music for $9.99/month. Revenue per stream: fractions of a cent.

Chapter 05 — The Paradox

More listeners.
Less money.

More people listen to more music than ever. Spotify has 600 million users. But total U.S. music revenue in 2019 was still less than half its 2000 peak.

~$0.004 avg payout per stream Revenue 45% below 2000 peak Vinyl outsells CDs since 2020

Fin

The disc is gone.
The record lives on.

The compact disc ruled for two decades and funded the most profitable era in music history. It's now nearly extinct — outsold by the format it once buried: vinyl.
