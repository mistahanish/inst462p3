Chapter 01 — Before the Disc

A world of vinyl and tape

Before the CD existed, the music industry was a battle of formats. Vinyl dominated the 1970s. 8-track cartridges surged, then died. By the early 1980s, the cassette and the Walkman had made portable music a reality for a generation.

Then in 1982, a silver disc changed everything.

Vinyl ~50%+ share through 1984 Cassette overtook Vinyl in 1984 8-Track dead by 1982

Chapter 02 — The Silver Invasion

The CD takes over

When Sony and Philips launched the compact disc in 1982, it was a curiosity. By 1987 it had overtaken vinyl. By 1991 it outsold cassettes. The sound was "perfect." Labels re-sold entire back catalogs to fans who already owned the albums — on CD.

Chapter 03 — The Peak

2000: $13 billion on discs alone

By 2002 the CD had captured nearly all physical music revenue — the most dominant any single format has ever been in U.S. history. The industry was making obscene money. Then a college dropout named Shawn Fanning launched Napster.

Chapter 04 — Three Killers

What killed the disc

The CD's collapse wasn't one event — it was a cascade. Three technologies, one after another, stripped away the economics that made CDs so profitable.

1999

Napster

Peer-to-peer file sharing made music free. CD sales fell 20% in two years. The labels sued — and won — but the damage was done.

2003

iTunes Store

Apple made paying cool again — but at $0.99 per song, not $15 per album. Revenue per listener collapsed. The album era ended.

2011

Spotify US

All the music ever made for $9.99/month. Downloads collapsed. The CD became a rounding error. Revenue per stream: fractions of a cent.

Chapter 05 — The Paradox

More listeners.
Less money.

More people listen to more music today than ever. Spotify alone has 600 million users. But total U.S. music revenue in 2019 was still less than half its 2000 peak — even as listening hours multiplied.
